## 🚀 The Book in 3 Sentences

1. This book is about financial discipline. It does not give advice on what worked for the authors or a celebrity but rather talks with examples of how many common folks with sensible frugality and discipline (regardless of their job or source of income) have built a better financial future compared to their peers in the same line of work.

2. It cites examples of the lesser known people and uses data from their surveys to answer some of the fundamental personal finance questions like being a high-income earner vs being wealthy, attitude towards money, inheritance, etc.

3. The book dives into how economic outpatient care (their term) weakens the children and how parents so desperately want to succeed. Although it is a simple idea, it is not easy to not support your loved ones. This was one of my main learnings from the book.

#### Who Should Read It?

This book is for anyone who deals with money (earn or spend). So that is everyone. I feel the younger people (early twenties) could benefit more by building a financial discipline at an early age.

I will ask my children to read this book before they start their careers.

## ☘️ How the Book Changed Me

The book formalizes a lot of conventional financial wisdom I heard growing up in a traditional middle-class Indian household. I had not always paid attention to that, but while reading this book it brought back some of those memories and hence it resonated well with me.

Now as a (reasonably well-off) new parent in a first-world country, I have been thinking about how to best create a setup for our kids in the future so that they can relate to the hardships of the real world. Often times the privilege in the upbringing itself becomes a crutch to success, so I have often thought about what framework to use to make decisions on economic outpatient care of the children. This book offers some food for thought and that was more relevant learning for me.

It gives a solid framework on how to think about important financial questions of the household.

## 📒 Summary + Notes

The title may sound a little cheesy, but it means how common people like someone living next door can be financially strong even though they are not making a show of it. Often times we attribute being rich or affluent to someone who spends on lavish vacations, watches, cars, etc. but they usually take away from the wealth you would have acquired otherwise. As the book says, it is easy to purchase products that denote such superiority than be actually superior in your financial outlook.

Next, the book lays down a framework of how to think about your savings achievement so far as the function of your age. Once you start earning above a certain threshold, being wealthy often means that you are not spending the income you’re earning. One of the biggest expenses of our life is income tax. So you should aim to reduce your realized income (taxable) as a function of your net worth.

Finally, the book talks about the [[Inheritance and Upbringing]] of children in a wealthy household. Kids growing up in poorer families are acclimatized to economic hardships. They know the value of money and often the grit to break their chains. But being born into a wealthy family, kids often do not have that perspective towards money. This can impact how they spend money over their lifetime. So even if they become high-income earners in their lifetime, they may not accumulate proportionate wealth.

## ✏️ Highlights

- It is easier to purchase products that denote superiority than to be actually superior in economic achievement.

- Offense = Earn high income, Defense = Save your earnings. Wealth = Offense + Defense. [[Prolific Accumulator of Wealth]] (PAW) = 2 * (Age * Income / 10)

- Reduce your realized income (you pay taxes on your realized income). Your realized income should be less than 10% of your net worth.

- Once you’re in a high income bracket it matters less on how much you make than how much you have.

- Track what percentage of gross income is going to savings

- A household divided in its financial orientation is unlikely to accumulate significant wealth (it is very important that you and your spouse have similar financial values).

- High status items require more time, energy, and money for maintenance.

- Time, energy, and money are finite resources.

- Consider how much time and effort was put in a “bargain” deal?

- Was it worth it? Sometimes deals we consider bargain may not be a real bargain if we spend significant time and energy in getting it.

- Is it for a worthy investment?

- For any investment think about the following:

- How much realized income will it produce?

- Will it hold value?

- Are there any better investments?

- Money should never change your values. It is only a report card of how you’re doing.

- Teach honor - do not give gifts earmarked for consumption.

- Instill thrift and independence in children.

- Do not weaken the weak. This is context of economic outpatient cares parents often provide to kids who are not doing well in their careers.

- Do not compete with children about money.

- Eg, when I was your age…

- Teach kids to enhance their achievements and what they own.

- Immunize them by facing hardships at home so they are ready to face them in the real world.
