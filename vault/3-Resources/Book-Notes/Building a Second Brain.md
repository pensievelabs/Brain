## 🚀 The Book in 3 Sentences

1. Capture what resonates with you, Organize it so that information is searchable, Distill your knowledge so that your future self spends a fraction of time recalling it, and Express your knowledge (preferably in smaller chunks) so that you’re moving forward.

2. Any creative process is composed of two steps: divergence (collecting or spit-balling ideas and organizing them) and convergence (focusing on a smaller subset, distilling the essence, and executing it).

3. Make sure you have clearly stated project goals, incorporate weekly and monthly reviews of your work and responsibilities (personal or professional), and keep organizing and decluttering your digital workspace.  

## 🎨 Impressions

  I think this is a good starting book about personal knowledge management. Many of the suggestions in the book are mostly followed by most of us in some form or the other (the author calls that out as well). However, it does give a better formal framework and reasoning about it.

### Who Should Read It?

Given most of us are knowledge workers these days, it is a useful read for most of us who need to do knowledge management for personal or professional reasons.

## ☘️ How the Book Changed Me

- I liked how the author mentions that we should have only a few open projects with clearly stated goals at any given time. In the past, I have had several personal projects which have gone haywire without seeing them to completion. One of the reasons was constantly evolving goals as I learned more about the project. While that is a natural evolution of any project, it also prevents us from achieving any sense of completion. So defining clearly stated goals anchors the project direction and delivering in small IPs gives you a sense of completion (that things are moving forward). New goals can be a part of newer iterations.

- The other idea that resonated well with me is the consider the concept of distillation as a value-addition for my future self. We capture notes for our utility but often forget the last mile of distillation without which our future self would not have time or resources or access to the note we have captured.

- There are a few things I am trying to apply in my daily life: a weekly review of projects, tasks, etc., and a monthly review of projects, goals, etc. One of my goals for this year (and as a new parent) is to make the best use of time for things that have value for me. Being more deliberate in how I am spending my time and where I should look next is something I was motivated to focus on already.

# 📒 Summary + Notes

- The central theme of the book is to reduce the burden on our biological brain by using existing technology - like note-taking apps, calendars, task management tools, etc. The tasks related to remembering things are better delegated to existing technologies while our biological brain becomes free to connect these ideas, learn new things, etc. which is at the core of creating new things.

- The author describes the above framework as a second brain which like a smartphone is also an appendage to you. He describes three frameworks, one each for the second brain, notes organization, and note summarization/distillation. These are as below:

	1. [[CODE]]
	2. [[PARA]]
	3. [[Progressive Summarization]]

- CODE refers to Capture → Organize → Distill → Express.

- Capture only what resonates with you. Try to convey it in your own words, so that it sticks. Act as a curator of information (not a hoarder), so keep only that will have a future value.

- Organize in terms where the information will be actionable to you rather than where you got it from. This makes it more discoverable when you need it. Supplement the info with tags or any searchable keywords.

- Distill the notes you have to a central message so your future self does not have to spend the same amount of time to get the gist of the message. Consider your future self as your customer.

- Express the information you collect into small IPs (intellectual packets/information packets). These small IPs can act as a building block for future projects.

- For Capture, the author suggests maintaining a list of 12 projects (based on Feynman’s approach) and deciding every capture candidate against usefulness in those projects (with a clearly stated goal). If not and the idea still resonates with you then it goes into one of the ARA folders described below.

- PARA stands for Projects; Areas; Resources; Archives. It is a format in which you can store your notes on any medium - which comes into play in the Organize step above.

- [[Progressive Summarization]] is a part of Distill step where you layer notes which increasingly succinct summaries. A rule of thumb here would that each layer of the summary should be no longer than 20% of its previous layer. This helps you get a high-level view of your notes and then you can dive deeper into interesting components at any given time.

- Finally, the author talks about incorporating these things into your daily routine by introducing weekly, and monthly reviews. They help to declutter your digital workspace and help identify priorities, and pending projects or just let you step back and get a higher-level picture of your life.
